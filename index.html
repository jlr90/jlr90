< Project Idea />

Overview

The project is an application which enables Learner Drivers to log their practice time under various conditions, keep track of what areas need work, and easily view statistics and tips which will enable them to become more confident – and importantly – safer drivers.

This project aims to raise the standard for Australian Learner Drivers. Currently each state has its own rules and own requirements for learning to drive. The aim of this project is not to change laws to be uniform across the country, but rather to ensure a consistent and clear level of experience and practice each learner should achieve before driving on their own.

Possible logo

Motivation

The risk of being involved in a car accident increases significantly once a driver switches from supervised driving on their L-Plates, to unsupervised driving on their P-Plates. With more – and importantly better – practice, we can reduce this risk.

According to a survey from Roy Morgan research in 2016, 94% of Australians between 14 and 17 years old have a smart phone. The typical starting age for Learner Drivers is 16 years old, so it is reasonable to assume there is a market for this product.

There are currently similar apps on the market, however they are only for specific Australian states, vary in what they offer the user, and often have poor ratings. Some apps cover local rules, some apps are general spreadsheet style log books, and some apps offer mock driving tests. None combine all useful features into one convenient app.

When I was a learner driver, I had to keep a logbook of every trip I took for two years. This was tedious and the book was cumbersome to carry around. The app will provide a simple, digital solution, offering more functionality and better feedback.

Image taken from "https://mylicence.sa.gov.au/gls/the_facts"

Description

The initial aim is to have the app cover all Australian states and territories. As mentioned, each region has its own rules regarding learner drivers. Some of these, such as L-Plate specific speed limits, affect drivers from other states when they cross the border and may not be known. The app will allow users to select their current state or territory and have a relevant ruleset apply to their profile.

Users will need to create an account to get started and keep track of their details and progress. After that, information can be gathered automatically, or input manually in an ‘offline’ mode. The user can input their target hours before taking the final driving test and months/years until they want to take the test.
The app will calculate recommended driving patterns, offer suggestions, and give reminders to practice regularly and in different conditions, all with a basic aim of 120 hours total practice while on L-Plates.

Some of the features will rely on the use of the mobile device’s GPS tracking to record information on average speed, city driving versus highways, short trips versus long trips, and total kilometres driven. A weather API will also need to be used in order to track driving experience in wet and dry conditions. And of course, the local date and time will be recorded to keep track of day time versus night time driving experience, and total time spent driving.

The dashboard page of the app will display current statistics for the user, including total time spent driving, total kilometres, total day and night hours and total wet and dry hours. More importantly, it will also display recommendations for the user, such as a suggestion to get more practice at night or in wet conditions.

For long journeys, the user will be able to ‘plan a trip’. This will include suggested break locations, and relevant local rules to consider, such as speed limits in other states, or rules for driving with trams if the driver is entering Melbourne.

Another important consideration is the use of mobile devices while driving. Obviously, I don’t want to encourage people to use their phones while driving. There could be a screen-lock feature where you can set the app up, hit a ‘start trip’ button, and the app will lock the phone while in use. The advice would be to keep the mobile device in the glove compartment or somewhere it won’t be a distraction while driving.

Potential difficulties in creating this application could include funding. As there is no clear way to monetise the app (other than through potentially annoying advertising) the obvious way would be through pitching to relevant government departments for funding.

Tools and Technologies

As this is a mobile app, it needs to be developed with both Android and iOS operating systems in mind. While it could be developed individually for both, I believe coding at least the basic, minimum viable product, in React Native would be the most efficient way to start. This would also allow for the front-end design to be put together easily.

There would need to be a back-end to handle signing up or logging on, communicating with a weather API, possibly communicating with something like Google Maps API, and for managing storage on the user’s device.

Given the suggestion for the JavaScript library React Native to be used for the front-end, Node.JS is a possible pairing for the back-end. Any other common back-end language such as Java or Python could also be considered. And of course, SQL for database management of things such as local state or territory rules, global statistics and user information.
Information would need to be stored on a server somewhere, potentially AWS. Other than the user owning a mobile device, there is no hardware requirement for this project.

Skills Required

The app would not require a particularly advanced skillset to create. There are many developers who would be capable of designing and building such an app.
A small team could include a confident JavaScript developer, someone with experience building mobile apps in React Native, a designer to consider the best layout and UI/UX, a back-end developer with API experience, and maybe someone to look after security or server-side details. Given the app is tracking the user’s location and could potentially collect data on regular movement patterns, security is an important consideration.

Outcome

A successful outcome would be for local governments to get behind the app and promote it as a useful learning tool for learner drivers. Confident and experienced drivers are what make our roads a safer place.
I want the app’s users to be comfortable driving in any condition. Hopefully the app can contribute to saving the lives of younger drivers once they are out on their own.

Future considerations could include an international version.
